# Question bank

Questions to draw from — not a script to run top-to-bottom. Pick the one that
goes one layer deeper than the last answer. Always pull abstractions back to a
concrete moment.

## Orientation (a quick read before you dig — light, not an intake form)
One short question to get basic context so you're not flying blind. Keep it to
stage + field + what's on the table; don't drift into logistics/constraints yet.
- "Before we dig in — give me the quick version: where are you right now (what
  year / about to graduate / working), in what field, and what are the options
  you're torn between?"
- "What's the situation in one breath — and what are people (or you) currently
  weighing?"

## Opening (flow / peak moment)
- "Think of a time in the last year you lost all track of time doing something,
  and felt warm afterward. Describe the scene concretely — what were you doing,
  alone or with others, and which *part* of it was the part you couldn't put down?"
- "When was the last time you felt genuinely *alive* in your work or studies?
  What exactly was happening in that moment?"

## Going deeper on a flow moment (separate the sub-driver)
Offer concrete candidates and make them choose + explain *why that one and not
the others*:
- the hunt for the perfect piece/answer (the "yes, that's it" hit)
- arranging chaos into something that finally feels *right* (composition/order)
- inventing something no one's done (originality)
- making it better than everyone else (standards / competition)
- finishing — the closure itself
What they *don't* pick is as informative as what they do.

## Energy drains (the inverse signal)
- "Across the last few years, which kind of task makes you want to flee the
  moment it lands on your desk? Give me the most honest one."
- "What have you started, been excited about, and then abandoned? Why did you
  stop?" (Mines the motivation-type and completion pattern.)

## Counterfactual tests
- Motivation type: "If literally no one would ever see this — zero likes, zero
  feedback, just you alone — would you still finish it?"
- Audience vs. standard: "When you say you were 'better than most,' what's the
  part that actually felt good — being *seen* as the best, *knowing* you were,
  or *beating a specific rival*?"

## Archetype-separating forks
- Builder/operator/technical: "When you 'vibe' for a whole day, what are you
  actually doing — building a real working thing, refining an idea or plan, or
  wrestling a hard technical problem for its own sake?"
- "Which would you rather: ship a rough thing people can use today, or perfect
  one hard piece no one else can crack?"

## Competitive orientation
- "When a close friend outperforms you, what's the honest inner experience?"
- "Do you tend to know roughly where you rank among people doing what you do? Does
  that ranking matter to you?"

## Collaboration style
- "Tell me about something you built or ran *with* someone. Who drove it, who
  caught the things that would've dropped? Did the partnership feel like fuel or
  friction?"

## Values / mission anchor
- "Why did you get into this field in the first place — the real reason, not the
  application-essay one?"
- "If money and difficulty were equal, which problem in the world would you most
  want to have spent your life on?"
Handle heavy answers with weight and care before using them.

## Constraint-gathering (do before recommending — NOT at the start; these gate everything)
Placement matters: basic context goes up front (Orientation, above); these harder
constraints come *here*, just before mapping — front-loading logistics early kills
the discovery.
- Location & work authorization (country, visa status, willingness to relocate).
- Time horizon (graduation date, runway, urgency).
- Money / risk tolerance / family obligations.
- Hard nos — "what would you refuse to do, even for good pay?"
- What "good" means to them — growth, salary, stability, autonomy, impact —
  ranked.

## When they stall / won't choose
- "You keep saying you haven't decided. Here's the thing — you just told me
  open-ended things make you fizzle out. So us circling forever is exactly the
  kind of thing you'd bail on. Let's give your wiring what it needs: a concrete
  step. Pick one of these — [structured choice]."
- Use their own diagnosed psychology as the reason to move, then offer 2-3
  bounded options rather than an open field.
