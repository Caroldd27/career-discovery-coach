# Archetypes & signals — a reading guide

These are *hypotheses to test*, never labels to assign. Read them from concrete
stories and confirm with counterfactuals before building on them.

## Motivation type: internally-sustained vs. externally-triggered
The single most decision-shaping read.

- **Internally-sustained:** grinds with no audience; finishes solo, open-ended
  things; intrinsic standards carry them.
- **Externally-triggered:** enormous drive *when* there's response, stakes,
  deadlines, or someone watching; abandons solo, no-feedback projects — not from
  laziness but because the fuel (external response) is absent.

**Signals of externally-triggered:** "I finished it because people were
watching / it got traction"; a graveyard of half-done private projects next to
one polished public one; energy spikes around competition or an audience.

**Counterfactual test:** "If literally no one would ever see this, would you
finish it?" A fast "no" is diagnostic.

**Implication:** externally-triggered people should *not* take solo, slow-feedback,
no-audience work (classic academia, lone indie hacking, long R&D with deferred
payoff). They thrive with teams, deadlines, visible users, competition. Their
"willpower problem" is solved by *environment design*, not by trying harder.

## Maker archetype: builder vs. operator vs. deep-technical
What they actually want to *do* all day. Most people lean one way; the first job
should match.

- **Builder:** energized by *making a working thing exist* — ships prototypes,
  tools, products; tolerates messiness to get to a result. In 2020s terms, often
  AI-native / no-code builders who don't need (or want) deep CS.
- **Operator:** energized by *ideas, decisions, and people* — analyzing a market,
  persuading, allocating, pushing a thing through an organization.
- **Deep-technical:** energized by *cracking a hard technical problem*, even
  slowly; will go deep where builders and operators get bored.

**How to separate:** ask what they do when they "vibe" for a whole day. Building a
real artifact → builder. Refining an idea/plan/strategy → operator. Wrestling a
gnarly technical problem for its own sake → deep-technical. Watch what they *rule
out* — someone who says "deep technical grind is definitely not me" has told you
a lot.

**First-job implication:** builders → product/prototyping/early-stage roles;
operators → product management, strategy, BD, consulting, founding; deep-technical
→ research/engineering ICs. A builder forced into a slow IC research seat will
burn out the same way the externally-triggered person does in solitude.

## Competitive orientation
- **Arena-driven:** instinctively ranks themselves in a field ("better than 99%
  of…"), suffers a little even when a friend they love outperforms them, is honest
  about who's genuinely better. Needs environments where they can compete, see the
  ladder, and climb.
- **Collaborative/mastery:** measures against their own past self, indifferent to
  ranking. Thrives in non-zero-sum, craft-deep settings.

Arena-driven people wilt in flat, non-competitive, "everyone's equal" environments
and come alive where there's a scoreboard.

## Audience / feedback dependence
Related to but distinct from motivation type. Some people's *fuel* is the response
itself (praise, reach, the scoreboard); for others the audience is just the
*arena* that makes the work worth doing, while the fuel is hitting their own bar.
Tease these apart — it changes whether they need a spotlight role or just real
stakes.

## The principal-vs-peer collaboration style
When they've worked with others, were they the **principal who delegates** (others
fill their gaps; they hold the decisions) or a **peer who co-drives** (two equals
pushing each other)? Principals often find equal co-founders frustrating and do
better as the decider with complementary executors. This matters a lot for founder
advice.

## Mission anchor
The formative "why" (see psychology-frameworks #10). When present and real, it's
the strongest tiebreaker between paths and the fuel for hard stretches. Look for a
through-line connecting their field, their values, and a formative experience —
and check whether their current path actually serves it.

## Reframing "flaws" as fit signals
Most self-described defects are person-environment mismatches:

- **"I have ADHD / no patience"** → needs fast feedback, novelty, urgency,
  multi-threading; wilts in repetition and slow payoff. Not a defect — a
  fit constraint. In the right environment the same wiring is hyperfocus.
- **"I'm flaky / never finish"** → usually externally-triggered + high-Openness;
  finishes fine *with* external structure.
- **"Too many scattered hobbies"** → breadth + a builder's itch; raw material for
  a generalist/operator/founder, not a problem to fix.

## Credential–wiring mismatch
A frequent root of "I'm lost": the person's *credential/default track* points one
way while their *behavior* points another (e.g., a science PhD whose every
energizing story is commercial/entrepreneurial). Name it directly — relieving this
specific dissonance is often the unlock of the whole session.
