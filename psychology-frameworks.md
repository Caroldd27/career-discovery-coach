# Psychology frameworks

Use these *under the hood* — to design sharper questions and interpret answers —
not as lectures to recite at the user. Each entry says what it is, what it tells
you, and how to apply it in a discovery conversation.

## 1. Flow theory (Csikszentmihalyi)
**What it is:** Flow is the state of full absorption in an activity — time
distorts, self-consciousness drops — occurring when challenge and skill are both
high and matched. **What it tells you:** flow moments are the least fakeable
signal of *intrinsic* engagement. People can rationalize what they "should" like;
they can't fake what made them lose track of time. **How to apply:** open with a
peak/flow question. Then drill into *which sub-component* produced the flow — the
craft, the problem-solving, the social impact, the winning, the building. Two
people can love "making stickers" for completely different reasons.

## 2. Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)
**What it is:** humans are intrinsically motivated when three needs are met —
**autonomy** (volition), **competence** (mastery), **relatedness** (connection).
Motivation runs on a spectrum from intrinsic to extrinsic (external reward, social
approval, internalized values). **What it tells you:** *why* an activity energizes
or drains. Someone starved of autonomy will wilt in a tightly-managed role no
matter the pay. **How to apply:** distinguish people whose drive is
**internally-sustained** (they'll grind with no audience) from those who are
**externally-triggered** (they need response, stakes, or accountability to
sustain effort). This single distinction reshapes the entire job recommendation —
see `archetypes-and-signals.md`. Counterfactual questions ("if no one ever saw
it…") expose it.

## 3. Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick)
**What it is:** a clinical method for helping people find their *own* motivation
to change, originally for behavior change. Core skills = **OARS**: Open questions,
Affirmations, Reflections, Summaries. Central discipline: resist the **"righting
reflex"** — the urge to jump in and fix/advise — because premature advice makes
people defend the status quo. **What it tells you / how to apply:** this is the
*conversational engine* of the whole skill. Ask open questions, reflect what you
hear (often the person hears their own truth only when you mirror it), evoke their
insight rather than installing yours, and roll with resistance instead of pushing.
It's why the method is questions-first and why "reflect → test → revise" beats
"diagnose → prescribe."

## 4. RIASEC / Holland Codes
**What it is:** six vocational-interest types — **R**ealistic (hands-on, things),
**I**nvestigative (analyze, ideas), **A**rtistic (create, express), **S**ocial
(help, teach), **E**nterprising (persuade, lead, sell), **C**onventional
(organize, detail). Most people are a blend of 2-3. **What it tells you:** a quick
coordinate system for interest direction. **How to apply:** infer their code from
stories rather than asking them to self-rate. Note especially Enterprising +
Artistic/Investigative blends — common in builder/operator types who don't fit a
single bucket. Use it to *generate hypotheses*, not to label.

## 5. Big Five (OCEAN)
**What it is:** the most empirically robust personality model — **O**penness,
**C**onscientiousness, **E**xtraversion, **A**greeableness, **N**euroticism.
**What it tells you:** broad tendencies (e.g., high Openness → novelty-seeking,
poor fit for repetitive work; the interaction of high Openness + lower
Conscientiousness often looks like "lots of unfinished projects"). **How to
apply:** read it lightly from behavior; don't pathologize. Low task-completion is
often high-Openness + externally-triggered motivation, not a character defect.

## 6. Ikigai
**What it is:** a popular framing (loosely adapted) of four overlapping circles —
what you **love**, what you're **good at**, what the world **needs**, and what you
can be **paid for**. **What it tells you:** a checklist for a *sustainable*
direction — passion without pay burns out; pay without meaning rots. **How to
apply:** use it to make sure a recommendation isn't missing a circle. The "world
needs / mission" circle is often the formative-wound anchor; the "paid for" circle
is the realism check the user came to you for.

## 7. Schein's Career Anchors
**What it is:** eight deep value-sets that, when understood, a person won't give
up: Technical/Functional competence, General Managerial competence, Autonomy/
Independence, Security/Stability, Entrepreneurial Creativity, Service/Dedication
to a cause, Pure Challenge, Lifestyle. **What it tells you:** the *non-negotiable*
underneath surface preferences — e.g., someone with an Autonomy anchor will be
miserable as a cog however prestigious the company. **How to apply:** identify the
1-2 anchors and weight recommendations toward roles that honor them. Entrepreneurial
Creativity + Pure Challenge + Autonomy is a founder signature.

## 8. Job Crafting (Wrzesniewski & Dutton)
**What it is:** the finding that people can reshape a given job — its tasks,
relationships, and the meaning they assign it — to better fit who they are.
**What it tells you / how to apply:** a person isn't trapped by a job title. If a
"safe" launchpad role is the realistic move, show how they can craft it toward
their bedrock (e.g., a field-support role crafted toward product). Useful for
sequencing launchpad → destination.

## 9. Strengths-based / Zone of Genius
**What it is:** the strengths movement (e.g., Gallup CliftonStrengths) and the
popular "Zone of Genius" (Hendricks) — the intersection of what you're uniquely
good at *and* energized by, distinct from what you're merely competent at.
**What it tells you / how to apply:** competence ≠ fit. People often have a
"Zone of Excellence" (good at it, drains them) they mistake for their path. Probe
for the difference: what are you good at that *also* lights you up, vs. good at
that *costs* you energy?

## 10. Formative narrative & values roots
**What it is:** people's deepest motivations are frequently rooted in formative
experiences — often a loss, an injustice, or a wound. **What it tells you:** the
"why" that can sustain someone through hard things. **How to apply:** when it
surfaces, slow down and honor it before connecting it to direction (see SKILL.md
ethics). A real mission anchor is the strongest fuel a career can have — and the
strongest tiebreaker between otherwise-similar paths.
